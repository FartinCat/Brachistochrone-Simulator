Theory & Derivation

The word Brachistochrone comes from the Greek: brachistos (shortest) + chronos (time). The problem, posed by Johann Bernoulli in 1696, asks: "What is the curve of fastest descent between two points under constant gravity?"

Intuitively, one might guess the answer is a straight line (shortest distance) or a steep arc that picks up speed quickly. Surprisingly, the answer is a cycloid — the curve traced by a point on the rim of a rolling circle.

The problem was solved in 1696 by Johann Bernoulli, Jacob Bernoulli, Newton, Leibniz, and de l'Hôpital — five correct solutions in one year — and became the founding problem of the Calculus of Variations.

The Brachistochrone is a cycloid, defined parametrically by:

x(θ) = R (θ − sin θ) y(θ) = R (1 − cos θ) where R = H / 2 (for a half-cycloid, θ ∈ [0, π]) H = vertical drop from start to end

The parameter θ is the rolling angle of the generating circle of radius R. At θ = 0, the ball is at the start (0, 0). At θ = π, it reaches the lowest point of the arc at (πR, 2R), which is our endpoint where y = H.

Cycloid (Brachistochrone):

Using energy conservation v = √(2gy) and the arc-length element of a cycloid, the transit time integrates exactly to:

T_cycloid = π √(R / g) where R = H/2, g = gravitational acceleration

Straight Line:

Along a straight incline of length L = √(X² + H²) at angle α = arctan(H/X):

T_straight = √( 2L² / (g·H) ) = √( 2(X² + H²) / (g·H) ) where X = π·H/2 (horizontal reach of the cycloid endpoint)

Circular Arc:

For the circular arc of radius Rc = (X² + H²) / (2H) with vertical tangent at start, no closed form exists, so we use numerical integration:

T_arc = ∫ ds / v(s) = ∫ ds / √(2g·y(s)) Evaluated by Riemann sum over N = 600 arc segments.

We want to minimize the time functional. Using energy conservation, the speed at depth y is v = √(2gy). The time along a curve y(x) is:

T = ∫ ds/v = ∫ √(1 + y'²) / √(2gy) dx

This is a functional of the form T = ∫ F(y, y') dx where F does not depend explicitly on x. The Euler-Lagrange equation then reduces to the Beltrami identity:

F − y' · (∂F/∂y') = C (constant) Substituting F = √(1 + y'²) / √(2gy) and simplifying: y (1 + y'²) = 1/(2gC²) = k (constant)

This is the differential equation of a cycloid, confirming that the brachistochrone is indeed a cycloid.

The cycloid possesses a remarkable second property, discovered by Huygens (1659): it is also a tautochrone (Greek: tauto = same, chronos = time).

This means: a ball released from any point on a cycloid bowl reaches the lowest point in exactly the same time, regardless of its starting position. This time is:

T_tautochrone = π √(R / g) (Identical to the brachistochrone transit time!)

Huygens used this property to design the first isochronous pendulum clock — a pendulum constrained by cycloidal cheeks to swing in a cycloidal arc, ensuring a constant period regardless of amplitude.

For a standard drop of H = 2.0 m on Earth (g = 9.81 m/s²):

R = H/2 = 1.000 m T_cycloid = π√(R/g) = π × √(1.000/9.81) ≈ 1.0028 s X_end = πR = 3.1416 m L = √(X²+H²) = √(9.870+4.000) = 3.723 m T_straight = √(2L²/gH) = √(27.721/19.62) ≈ 1.1888 s T_arc = (numerical) ≈ 1.0782 s Ranking: T_cycloid < T_arc < T_straight 1.0028 s < 1.078 s < 1.189 s

The cycloid is approximately 18.6% faster than the straight line and 7.5% faster than the circular arc for these parameters. These ratios change with H and g but the ranking always holds: Cycloid < Arc < Straight.
